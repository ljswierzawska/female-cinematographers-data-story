Cinematography is a Man's World

By Luiza Swierzawska | March 22, 2026

Being a director of photography has overwhelmingly been seen as a "man's" career. Academy Award nominations alone reflect the underrepresentation of women in this category.

On March 15, Autumn Durald Arkapaw made history as she climbed the stage at the Dolby Theatre in Hollywood to accept the Academy Award for Best Cinematography. The 46-year-old director of photography became the first woman to win the film industry’s most prestigious award in this category, for her work on Sinners — one of 2025’s most celebrated films. While the evening was filled with glamour and viral celebrity moments, this recognition may stand out as the most noteworthy

Historically, women have been largely absent from the race for Best Cinematography, rarely even nominated. The first woman to receive such recognition was Rachel Morrison for her work on Mudbound in 2017, at the 90th Academy Awards.

That milestone was followed by only two more nominations: Ari Wegner for The Power of the Dog in 2021, and Mandy Walker for Elvis in 2022. Then came Arkapaw’s win.

And that’s not the only record she set that night. Arkapaw is also the first woman of color to be nominated for and to win in this category.

While accepting the Hollywood’s favorite award, Arkapaw thanked the women who supported her work and paid tribute to cinematographers who influenced her — Rachel Morrison and Ellen Kuras. She then asked the women in the room to stand. “I feel like I don’t get here without you guys,” she said, as the crowd rose in applause.

This gender imbalance extends far beyond awards season.

Until 1980, the American Society of Cinematographers — a prestigious Hollywood organization that brings cinematographers together — had no female members. Brianne Murphy became the first woman to join, 61 years after the organization was founded.

The society’s current president is Mandy Walker — one of 4 women in the Oscars history to compete in Best Cinematography.

Female Cinematographers in Top-Grossing Films Are Rare

Last year saw a decline in the already low percentage of female directors of photography working on top 250 grossing U.S. films.

12%

The Celluloid Ceiling report found that in 2025, only 7% of cinematographers working on top-grossing U.S. films were women. It’s a drop of five percentage points from the year before.

 

In total, women made up 23% of directors, writers, producers, executive producers, editors, and cinematographers working on the 250 top-grossing films in 2025, a figure unchanged from 2024.

With the film industry on increasingly uncertain ground — with AI threatening creatives and the politics threatening diversity — this is a grim perspective.

Arkapaw, Walker, Murphy, Morrison and others are rewriting the decades of little to no recognition of the female work in cinematography, in an industry that continues to face both external pressures and the need for internal change.
